# Optionality Within the Two Pillars

The two pillars are non-negotiable. What is flexible — and what the director gets to decide — is how each pillar is built. Present these as a decision spectrum, not a menu of competing proposals.

The framing for the director: *"These are the decisions that are yours to make. We have a recommendation, but the right answer depends on your budget, your teachers, and how fast you want to move."*

---

## Pillar 1 — Infrastructure Options

### Option A — Low-Tech (Google Drive / Microsoft 365)
**What it is:** A structured folder architecture with standardized naming conventions, shared template files, and a master alignment tracker (a spreadsheet mapping units to competencies). Everything lives in tools teachers already use.

**Pros:**
- Zero additional cost
- No learning curve for teachers or administrators
- Immediately deployable
- Familiar to everyone

**Cons:**
- Structurally fragile — degrades over time without human discipline to maintain it
- No built-in alignment reporting or cross-program visibility
- Hard to search across programs or campuses at scale
- Version control is manual and error-prone

**Best fit if:**
- Budget is tight or unavailable for new tools
- You want to prove the curriculum model before investing in a platform
- Speed of deployment is the priority

---

### Option B — Mid-Tech (Notion / Airtable or similar)
**What it is:** A relational workspace where units, competencies, and assessments are linked rather than just filed. Dashboards can show alignment at a glance, filter by program or campus, and give leadership a navigable view of curriculum without a spreadsheet.

**Pros:**
- Low cost (free tiers available; paid tiers modest)
- Significantly more powerful than folders — relational structure enables cross-referencing
- Flexible enough to adapt as the model evolves
- Visually navigable for teachers and leaders

**Cons:**
- Requires someone to design and maintain the workspace architecture
- Not purpose-built for curriculum — some workarounds needed
- Adoption requires onboarding even if the tools are intuitive

**Best fit if:**
- There is some internal tech-forward capacity to build and maintain the workspace
- You want more structural power without a full SaaS commitment
- The organization wants to pilot before committing to a dedicated platform

---

### Option C — Purpose-Built Curriculum Platform (Atlas, Chalk, Toddle, Curriculum Trak)
**What it is:** Dedicated curriculum mapping software designed for exactly this use case — built-in alignment to standards and competency frameworks, cross-campus visibility, teacher-facing curriculum authoring, leadership reporting.

**Platform notes:**
- **Atlas (Rubicon):** Strong UbD alignment, widely used in K-12, good cross-campus reporting
- **Chalk:** Clean teacher-facing UI, strong standards alignment, good for CTE competency mapping
- **Toddle:** Strong collaborative unit planning features, more internationally oriented but growing in US
- **Curriculum Trak:** Purpose-built for CTE and faith-based schools, direct competency alignment features

**Pros:**
- Highest long-term ceiling
- Most sustainable without internal maintenance burden
- Reporting and alignment visibility built in
- Purpose-designed for curriculum authoring and management

**Cons:**
- Real cost — typically per-seat or institutional licensing
- Requires onboarding and adoption effort
- Can feel like overkill if the writing process isn't solid first
- Vendor dependency

**Best fit if:**
- The district is ready to make a platform investment
- Long-term sustainability and scalability are priorities
- Leadership wants reporting capability without building it manually

---

### Recommendation
**Start with Option A or B depending on platform appetite**, with a clear intention to migrate to Option C once the curriculum writing process is established and the model is proven. The risk of starting with a platform before the process is solid is that the platform becomes an expensive empty shell.

The non-negotiable: the template structure, competency alignment logic, and naming conventions are defined independent of the platform and carry over regardless of which option is chosen or when migration occurs.

## Pillar 2 — Writing Protocol Options

### Option A — DACUM-Primary
**What it is:** The process starts and largely stays with occupational task analysis. Teachers and industry partners identify and validate what a competent worker does. Tasks cluster into units. Units map to credentials. Assessments are performance-based. UbD concepts are introduced lightly — enduring understandings and essential questions appear as reflection prompts rather than structural requirements.

**Pros:**
- Meets CTE teachers exactly where they already think — in tasks, skills, and competencies
- Familiar framework for vocational and trades programs
- Directly tied to credential alignment
- Lower conceptual lift for teachers new to formal curriculum writing

**Cons:**
- Can produce units that are thorough but thin on transfer — students learn to do things without understanding why
- May not satisfy instructional quality expectations surfaced in an SREB audit
- Less defensible as a pedagogical framework in conversations with state or accrediting bodies

**Best fit if:**
- Teachers are skeptical of education frameworks and need a practical entry point
- The priority is credential alignment above instructional coherence
- The program is highly skills-intensive (building trades, welding, auto tech)
- Speed is a priority and deeper PL investment is not yet feasible

---

### Option B — DACUM/UbD Hybrid (Recommended)
**What it is:** Task analysis provides the skeleton — what competent workers do, validated by the field. UbD provides the architecture on top. Each cluster of tasks becomes a unit with an enduring understanding, essential questions, and a defined performance assessment before instruction is planned. The language is industry-grounded but the structure is pedagogically coherent.

**The synthesis:** DACUM gives you the *what*. UbD gives you the *how* and *why*.

**Unit development sequence:**
1. Task analysis / competency mapping (DACUM-informed) → verified task list organized by duty area
2. Cluster tasks into units; identify enduring understanding for each cluster (UbD Stage 1)
3. Define performance assessment evidence, including credential alignment (UbD Stage 2)
4. Build instructional sequence and learning activities (UbD Stage 3)
5. House in infrastructure

**Pros:**
- Meets teachers where they are (task-based) while building toward something more coherent
- Satisfies both CTE credentialing requirements and instructional quality standards
- Enduring understandings answer the question CTE students most need answered: *"Why does this matter in the field?"*
- Defensible in SREB, state, and accreditation contexts

**Cons:**
- More facilitation skill required to run well
- Requires the foundational PL session to work — teachers need the conceptual framework first
- Takes longer than Option A to produce finished units

**Best fit if:**
- The goal is curriculum that is both vocationally credible and instructionally coherent
- The SREB audit findings are taken seriously as a driver of change
- The organization is willing to invest in the PL arc alongside the writing process
- This is the recommended approach for most CTC programs

---

### Option C — UbD-Primary with CTE Adaptations
**What it is:** Full backward design from the start. Enduring understandings are drawn from industry standards and credential frameworks rather than academic disciplines. Essential questions are written in industry-relevant language. The process is more formally pedagogical but grounded in CTE context throughout.

**Pros:**
- Produces the most pedagogically coherent curriculum
- Strong alignment with instructional quality frameworks
- Teachers who engage fully develop the deepest curriculum thinking

**Cons:**
- Can feel abstract to trades-oriented teachers without very careful facilitation
- Highest conceptual lift — requires the most robust foundational PL
- Risk of alienating teachers who are skeptical of "education jargon"

**Best fit if:**
- Teachers have prior UbD exposure or come from a more academically adjacent background
- The programs being developed are academically oriented (Health Sciences, IT/Cybersecurity, Business, Early Childhood)
- The facilitator has strong UbD fluency and CTE contextual knowledge

---

### Recommendation
**Option B — the hybrid — is the recommended approach for a CTC context.** It is the honest best fit: it respects where CTE teachers live intellectually, builds on the DACUM tradition that occupational education has trusted for decades, and adds the pedagogical architecture that SREB audits and instructional quality conversations require. It is also the most defensible choice if curriculum quality ever comes under external review.
