# SREB Audit — Context and Meeting Use

## What the Audit Represents Strategically

The SREB audit is the single most important asset in this meeting. It reframes the entire conversation: rather than arriving as consultants asking the director to diagnose his own problems, you arrive having already documented what is happening instructionally — from a credible, external third-party source.

This shifts the dynamic from *"let us help you figure out what's wrong"* to *"we already know what's happening — here's what it means, and here's what we'd do about it."*

SREB (Southern Regional Education Board) carries significant credibility in career and technical education contexts. Its audit findings are not easily dismissed.

---

## How to Use Audit Artifacts in the Meeting

### The curriculum artifact moment (Segment 2)
Identify 1–2 examples from the audit that best illustrate the current state of curriculum documentation — a lesson plan, a curriculum map, a pacing guide, or a task sheet. Choose examples that are representative rather than outliers (the goal is not to embarrass anyone).

Place the artifact on the table or bring it up on screen during the grounding segment. Do not editorialize. Let it sit for a moment before continuing. The artifact does the work.

The implicit message: *"This is what curriculum looks like right now. We're not speculating."*

### What to avoid
- Do not use the artifact to criticize individual teachers
- Do not spend time analyzing the artifact in the meeting — it is a prop, not an agenda item
- Do not show more than one or two examples — the point lands quickly

## Likely Audit Findings to Reference
*(Populate with actual findings — the following are common SREB CTE audit themes)*

**Instructional consistency:**
- Significant variation in instructional approach and rigor across campuses and programs
- Instruction heavily reliant on demonstration and task repetition without explicit connection to larger competency goals

**Curriculum documentation:**
- Curriculum not formally documented in a consistent format
- Task sheets and textbooks functioning as de facto curriculum rather than teacher-authored units
- No shared understanding of what a "unit" means across the faculty

**Alignment:**
- Inconsistent or informal alignment to industry credentials (NOCTI, OSHA, certifications)
- No visible mapping of instruction to state CTE standards or program competencies

**Cross-campus consistency:**
- Same programs taught differently across campuses with no shared scope or sequence
- No mechanism for teachers across campuses to share, align, or update curriculum

## The Focus Group Finding

Separate from but connected to the SREB audit: teacher focus groups identified that newer teachers lack a clear mental model of the unit/lesson distinction.

**How to frame this in the meeting:**
*"One thing that came through clearly in focus groups is that newer teachers don't have a shared mental model of what a unit is versus a lesson — they're often working at the lesson level without a larger arc organizing their instruction."*

**Why this matters:**
- It explains why a template alone won't work — you can't fill out a form you don't conceptually understand
- It adds a professional learning dimension to the proposal that is grounded in observed need, not assumption
- It is a finding the director can take back to a board or funding conversation as evidence of a concrete instructional problem

**What not to do:**
- Do not frame this as a criticism of teachers — frame it as a gap in onboarding and support
- Do not spend more than 2–3 sentences on it in the grounding segment — it gets fuller treatment in the Pillar 2 section

## Connecting Audit to Solution

The through-line you want the director to draw:

| Audit Finding | Pillar Response |
|---|---|
| No shared system for curriculum | Pillar 1 — Infrastructure |
| Inconsistency across campuses | Pillar 1 — Infrastructure (cross-campus template + alignment mapping) |
| Curriculum lives in teachers' heads | Pillar 1 — Infrastructure (living documents, not binders) |
| No shared writing process | Pillar 2 — Writing Protocol |
| Credential misalignment | Pillar 2 — DACUM task analysis anchored to credential frameworks |
| Teachers lack unit/lesson mental model | Pillar 2 — Foundational PL as load-bearing component |

You do not need to walk through this table explicitly in the meeting. The connections should feel logical and inevitable, not itemized.
