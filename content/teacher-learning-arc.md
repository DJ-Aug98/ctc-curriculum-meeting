# Teacher Learning Arc — Rationale and Design

## Why Professional Learning Is Load-Bearing

The focus group finding — that newer teachers do not have an internalized understanding of the unit/lesson relationship — changes the nature of this project. It is no longer sufficient to design a curriculum template and a writing process and hand them to teachers. The writing protocol and the professional learning design are inseparable.

A teacher who does not understand what a unit *is* will fill out a unit plan template the way they fill out any form — completing fields without the understanding that makes the form meaningful. The result is bureaucratic paperwork, not curriculum.

This means the project has three interconnected components, not two:
1. The infrastructure (where curriculum lives)
2. The writing protocol (how curriculum gets built)
3. The professional learning design (how teachers develop the understanding and skill to use the protocol)

The third component is not an add-on. Without it, the other two do not hold.

## The Unit/Lesson Distinction — What Teachers Need to Understand

### What a unit is
A unit is a coherent chunk of learning organized around:
- A competency cluster (a set of related tasks a worker performs)
- A big idea or enduring understanding (a transferable insight that outlasts the unit)
- A performance outcome (what a student will be able to do independently at the end)

A unit has a defined beginning, middle, and end. It moves students from introduction to increasingly complex application to independent performance. It is not a chapter, a week, a topic, or a collection of daily activities.

### What a lesson is within a unit
A lesson is a single instructional move in service of the unit's larger arc. It is one day, one activity, one skill introduction — meaningful in context but not sufficient alone. A lesson answers "what are we doing today?" A unit answers "where are we going, and why?"

### The CTE teacher's default position
Most CTE teachers, especially newer ones and those coming directly from industry, think in lessons by default. They are practitioners — they know how to do things and how to teach others to do things step by step. This is a strength. But it means they tend to build instruction from the bottom up: *here is what we will do today, here is what we will do tomorrow.*

The conceptual shift required is learning to design from the top down: *here is what students need to be able to do independently — what is the evidence of that, and what sequence of instruction leads there?* This is the essence of backward design.

### The DACUM bridge
DACUM task analysis is a useful conceptual bridge for CTE teachers because it starts where they already live. Most CTE teachers have strong intuitions about competency clusters — they know what a journeyman electrician, a licensed practical nurse, or a carpenter can do. They just haven't connected that knowledge to the idea of a *unit.*

The facilitation move is to help them see: the duty areas in a DACUM task inventory are essentially unit anchors. The tasks within a duty area are the competencies a unit builds toward. The synthesis is intuitive once the connection is made explicit.

## The Professional Learning Arc

### Phase 0 — Exemplar Immersion (within foundational session)
Before teachers write anything, they should see a finished exemplar unit — ideally from a program adjacent to their own, so it is credible without being something they feel they should have already written.

The exemplar review should surface:
- What the unit's enduring understanding or big idea is
- How the performance assessment defines the unit's target
- How individual lessons accumulate toward the performance
- What the template structure looks like when it is working

This is also where the sample unit plan template is introduced — teachers see the form in use before they are asked to use it.

### Phase 1 — Foundational PD Session (~2–3 hours, facilitated)
**Objectives:**
- Build shared vocabulary: unit, lesson, enduring understanding, essential question, performance assessment, competency, task
- Establish the unit/lesson relationship through CTE-grounded examples
- Connect DACUM task analysis to unit architecture
- Review the exemplar unit
- Preview the writing protocol they will use

**Design principles:**
- Facilitated, not lectured — teachers construct understanding, they don't receive it
- CTE-contextualized throughout — examples come from program areas teachers recognize
- Produces a small tangible output — teachers leave with a draft unit title or competency cluster identified for their program

### Phase 2 — Task Analysis Session (DACUM-informed, ~3 hours)
**Objectives:**
- Generate a verified task inventory for the pilot program
- Organize tasks by duty area (these become unit anchors)
- Map tasks to credential frameworks
- Identify priority units for the first writing cycle

**Facilitation notes:**
- Include industry partners if possible — their presence validates the task list
- Use a structured DACUM format: duty areas across the top, tasks listed below each
- Output should be a full task inventory document, not just a list

### Phase 3 — Collaborative Unit Writing (iterative, multiple sessions)
**Objectives:**
- Produce finished unit plans using the chosen protocol
- Build teacher fluency with the template and process
- Develop peer review norms for curriculum quality

**Session structure (per unit writing session, ~2–3 hours):**
1. Review task cluster from the inventory
2. Identify enduring understanding and essential questions (UbD Stage 1)
3. Define performance assessment (UbD Stage 2)
4. Build instructional sequence (UbD Stage 3)
5. Place finished unit into infrastructure
6. Brief peer review — does this unit do what it says it does?

**Design principle:** Every session ends with something written. Teachers do not leave empty-handed.

### Phase 4 — Sustainability and Teacher Leadership
Once the initial curriculum set is built, the goal is for teachers to own and run the process themselves — updating units, onboarding new teachers, and running task analyses for new or revised programs.

This requires:
- At least 1–2 teacher leaders per program who have deep process fluency
- A documented process guide teachers can use independently
- A leadership check-in structure (administrator review of curriculum updates on a defined cycle)

## Implication for the Meeting Pitch

When presenting Pillar 2, name the professional learning arc explicitly as part of the offer — not as an explanation, but as a design feature. The framing:

*"Because focus groups told us that the unit/lesson distinction isn't yet shared language in the building, we've designed the writing process so that the professional learning and the curriculum writing happen together. Teachers don't sit through training and then go write — they build understanding by doing the work."*

This is a meaningful differentiator. It signals that you've listened to what the organization actually needs, not just delivered a standard curriculum development package.
