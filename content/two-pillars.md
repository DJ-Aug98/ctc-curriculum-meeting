# The Two Pillars — Core Framework

These two pillars are the non-negotiables of the proposal. What is flexible is *how* each pillar is built — see `optionality.md` for the decision spectrum within each.

---

## Pillar 1 — Curriculum Infrastructure

### The Problem It Solves
Curriculum across the three campuses currently lives in binders, individual teacher drives, or in teachers' heads. There is no shared visibility, no consistency in structure, and no way for leadership to see what is actually being taught across programs. When a teacher leaves, the curriculum often leaves with them.

### What It Is
A structured, accessible home for curriculum that is:
- **Consistent** — every program, every campus uses the same template structure and competency alignment logic
- **Visible** — leadership can see what is being taught across programs and campuses without asking individual teachers
- **Living** — designed to be updated and improved, not produced once and filed away
- **Aligned** — every unit maps explicitly to industry competencies and credential frameworks

### What It Is Not
- A compliance exercise or documentation burden
- A platform decision first — the structure and logic must be defined before any tool is chosen
- A one-size-fits-all solution — the template accommodates different program types while maintaining consistency in its architecture

### The Non-Negotiable
Regardless of which infrastructure option is chosen (see `optionality.md`), the following elements remain consistent:
- Unit plan template structure and required fields
- Competency/standard alignment mapping
- Cross-campus naming and organizational conventions
- Leadership visibility into curriculum at the program level

## Pillar 2 — Curriculum Writing Protocol + Teacher Learning

### The Problem It Solves
Even with a structured home for curriculum, teachers need a shared process for building it. Focus groups identified that many newer teachers do not have an internalized understanding of the unit/lesson distinction — they operate at the lesson level without a larger instructional arc organizing their work. Handing teachers a template without addressing this first produces filled-out forms, not coherent curriculum.

The professional learning design is therefore load-bearing, not optional. The writing protocol and the teacher learning are inseparable.

### What It Is
A structured development process with three interconnected components:

#### Component 1 — Foundational Professional Learning
Before any writing begins, teachers need a shared conceptual framework for:
- What a **unit** is: a coherent chunk of learning organized around a competency cluster, a big idea, or a performance outcome — not a chapter, a week, or a topic
- What a **lesson** is *within* a unit: a single instructional move in service of the larger arc
- Why the distinction matters practically: teachers who don't internalize this write lessons when asked for units, and units that are really glorified lesson plans
- What a **finished unit looks like**: an exemplar review so teachers can see the target before they write toward it

Estimated time: 2–3 hours, facilitated
CTE bridge: DACUM task analysis connects to this naturally — most CTE teachers have strong intuitions about competency clusters even if they've never called them units. The facilitation move is helping them see that what they already know as "what a journeyman electrician can do" maps directly onto what a unit is trying to build.

#### Component 2 — Task Analysis (DACUM-informed)
Subject matter experts — teachers and industry partners — identify and validate what a competent worker does in a given program area. This produces a verified task list that becomes the skeleton of the curriculum.
- Organized by duty areas (broad competency domains) and tasks (specific observable skills)
- Validated against industry credential frameworks (NOCTI, etc.)
- Output: a task inventory that maps to credential requirements and becomes the anchor for unit development

#### Component 3 — Collaborative Unit Writing
Teachers draft units using the chosen protocol (see `optionality.md`) with facilitation support. The key design principle: **teachers leave each session with actual curriculum, not just training.** The PL and the writing are the same activity.

The arc of a writing session:
1. Review the task cluster (from the task analysis) that will anchor the unit
2. Identify the enduring understanding or big idea that organizes the competencies
3. Define the performance assessment that would demonstrate mastery
4. Build the instructional sequence backward from the assessment
5. Place the finished unit into the infrastructure

### The Professional Learning Arc (full sequence)
```
Foundational PD Session
        ↓
DACUM-style Task Analysis
        ↓
Collaborative Unit Writing (iterative — multiple sessions)
        ↓
Housing Output in Infrastructure
        ↓
Teacher-led updates over time
```

### Design Principles
- Teachers build the curriculum themselves — it belongs to them, not to a consultant
- Each working session produces a tangible artifact — no session ends without something written
- The process is designed to be internalized so teachers can eventually run it without external facilitation
- Consistency across campuses comes from the shared protocol and template, not from top-down mandates

### Connection to SREB Findings
The focus group finding — that newer teachers lack a mental model of unit vs. lesson — should be named explicitly in the meeting. It explains why the professional learning component is structural rather than supplemental, and it connects the audit findings directly to the solution being proposed.
