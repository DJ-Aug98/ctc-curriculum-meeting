What stays consistent
Whichever platform is chosen, the template structure and competency alignment logic travel with it. The platform serves the curriculum — not the other way around.
Option A — Infrastructure
Low-Tech: Google Drive / Microsoft 365
A structured folder architecture with standardized naming conventions, shared template files, and a master alignment tracker in a spreadsheet. Everything lives in tools teachers already have.
What it looks like
  • Folder hierarchy by campus, program, and unit
  • Shared unit plan templates (Docs / Word)
  • Master tracker mapping units to competencies
  • Defined permission levels by role
What it requires
  • Someone to design the folder architecture upfront
  • Naming convention discipline across all users
  • A designated keeper for the master tracker
  • Periodic audits to catch structural drift
Easier because
  • Zero additional cost
  • No learning curve — tools already in use
  • Deployable immediately
Harder because
  • Structure degrades without active maintenance
  • No built-in reporting or cross-campus visibility
  • Version control is manual
One way to think about it

Option A asks people to impose structure on a tool that wasn't designed for it. It works — but the structure lives in human behavior, not in the system. The question to sit with: does the organization have the discipline to maintain that over time, and what happens when people turn over?

Option B — Infrastructure
Mid-Tech: Notion / Airtable or similar
A relational workspace where units, competencies, and assessments are linked rather than just filed. Leadership can see curriculum across programs and campuses without a spreadsheet.
What it looks like
  • Relational database linking units, duty areas, and competencies
  • Views filterable by campus, program, or credential
  • Embedded unit plan template fields
  • Leadership view showing curriculum coverage at a glance
What it requires
  • Someone to design and maintain the workspace architecture
  • Onboarding for teachers and administrators
  • Ongoing stewardship as the model evolves
Easier because
  • Free tiers available; paid tiers modest
  • Relational structure makes cross-referencing possible
  • Flexible enough to grow as the model matures
Harder because
  • Not purpose-built for curriculum — workarounds needed
  • Requires internal technical capacity to build
  • Adoption requires intentional onboarding
One way to think about it

Option B gives you most of the structural benefit of a purpose-built platform at a fraction of the cost. The catch is that someone has to build and maintain the architecture. If that capacity exists — or can be developed — it's a strong middle path between "folder on a shared drive" and "enterprise software."

Option C — Infrastructure
Purpose-Built Platform
Dedicated curriculum mapping software designed for exactly this use case — built-in alignment to competency frameworks, cross-campus visibility, teacher-facing authoring, and leadership reporting.
Platforms to consider
  • Atlas — strong UbD alignment, cross-campus reporting
  • Chalk — clean teacher UI, strong CTE competency mapping
  • Curriculum Trak — purpose-built for CTE, direct competency alignment
  • Toddle — collaborative unit planning features
What it requires
  • Platform selection and procurement
  • Structured onboarding for teachers and administrators
  • Ongoing vendor relationship
  • A writing process worth housing before investing
Easier because
  • Built for this purpose — alignment and reporting included
  • Most sustainable long-term without internal maintenance
  • Leadership visibility built in, not bolted on
Harder because
  • Real per-seat or institutional licensing cost
  • Onboarding and change management required
  • A platform with no curriculum in it solves nothing
One way to think about it

Option C is likely the right long-term home — but possibly not the right starting point. A platform investment before the writing process is established tends to produce an expensive, empty shell. The stronger sequence might be: start with A or B to build and prove the process, then migrate once the investment is justified by what's already in it.

The relationship
UbD defines the destination — what students will understand and be able to do. PBL is how they get there. The frame and the delivery are designed to reinforce each other.
The Frame — Protocol
Understanding by Design
UbD is the design framework — backward design from desired outcomes. You start by defining what students will understand and be able to do, then design the evidence of that, then plan the learning. The instructional approach (how you get there) is a separate — and open — question.
The three stages
  • Stage 1 — Desired Results: What should students understand? What enduring insight or competency does this unit build toward? What are the essential questions that drive inquiry?
  • Stage 2 — Assessment Evidence: What would it look like if students actually got there? Define the performance task before planning any instruction.
  • Stage 3 — Learning Plan: Now design the instruction — backward from the assessment, in service of the enduring understanding.
Why it fits CTE
  • Industry competency standards are already outcome-defined — UbD gives them curriculum structure
  • Credential alignment lives naturally in Stage 2 — the performance task mirrors real industry work
  • Enduring understandings answer the question CTE students most need: "why does this matter in the field?"
  • The unit/lesson distinction becomes clear: lessons are Stage 3 moves in service of Stage 1
What it produces
  • Units that are coherent — every lesson connects to a larger purpose
  • Assessments that are authentic — designed before instruction, not after
  • Curriculum that is transferable — students understand the why, not just the how
  • A shared design language across programs and campuses
What it requires
  • Teachers need the conceptual foundation before the template makes sense
  • Facilitated writing sessions — not something teachers do alone with a blank form
  • Time investment upfront that pays off in curriculum quality and sustainability
One way to think about it

UbD doesn't tell you how to teach — it tells you how to design. Stage 3 is intentionally open: the learning plan could be a traditional instructional sequence, a project, a simulation, or something else entirely. The frame sets the destination; the delivery approach determines the journey.

The Delivery — Protocol
Project Based Learning
PBL is a delivery approach — a way of structuring Stage 3 of a UbD unit so that students learn by doing sustained, authentic work organized around a driving question. In a PBL unit, the project isn't an add-on at the end. It's the vehicle for all the learning that happens before it.
Core elements of PBL
  • Driving question: An open, authentic question that organizes the project and anchors the enduring understanding
  • Sustained inquiry: Students investigate, build knowledge, and apply it — not in a single lesson but over time
  • Authentic context: The work mirrors what practitioners actually do in the field
  • Public product: Something students create, present, or defend — the Stage 2 performance task made real
  • Reflection: Students think about what they learned and how they learned it
Why it fits CTE
  • CTE programs already have authentic contexts — the shop, the clinic, the kitchen, the studio
  • Industry-relevant products are natural PBL outputs
  • The driving question maps directly onto professional problem-solving
  • Students learn by doing — which is already how most CTE instruction works at its best
What it adds
  • Engagement — students work on something that matters, not just something that's assigned
  • Transfer — skills learned in a project context are more likely to stick
  • Coherence — the project gives every lesson a clear purpose within the unit arc
  • Credential alignment — the product can be the performance task, not a separate assessment
What to consider
  • Not every unit needs to be a full project — PBL exists on a spectrum
  • Project design requires teacher skill and facilitation — it's more than "give students a task"
  • The driving question is harder to write well than it looks
One way to think about it

PBL without UbD can produce engaging experiences that don't build toward anything coherent. UbD without PBL can produce coherent curriculum that isn't engaging or authentic. Together, they address both — UbD ensures the project is purposeful, PBL ensures the learning is real.

In Practice — Protocol
UbD + PBL in a CTE Unit
When UbD is the frame and PBL is the delivery, the design sequence is clear: backward from the enduring understanding to the project to the driving question to the learning arc. The project isn't assigned at the end of the unit — it's designed first, as the evidence of understanding.
How they map together
  • Stage 1 (UbD) → Driving question (PBL): The enduring understanding becomes the driving question that anchors the project
  • Stage 2 (UbD) → Public product (PBL): The performance task is the project deliverable — designed before any instruction is planned
  • Stage 3 (UbD) → Learning arc (PBL): The instructional sequence scaffolds students toward completing the project with genuine understanding
What this looks like in CTE
  • A Health Sciences unit where the project is a patient care plan — assessed against clinical standards
  • A Trades unit where the project is a built structure — evaluated against industry specifications
  • An IT unit where the project is a working system — tested against real-world requirements
  • In each case: the project is the curriculum, not an add-on to it
What it requires from teachers
  • Understanding both frameworks well enough to design the project before the lessons
  • Comfort with students working at different paces toward the same outcome
  • Facilitation skill — managing a project arc is different from managing a lesson sequence
The open question
  • Should every unit be PBL-delivered, or is PBL the design standard for certain unit types?
  • Which programs are best positioned to pilot this approach first?
  • What does the teacher learning sequence look like for teachers new to both frameworks?
One way to think about it

This combination is aspirational — and worth naming as such. The question for this conversation isn't "is this the right approach?" but "is this the direction we want to build toward?" The infrastructure and protocol can be designed to accommodate this from the start, even if the first units are simpler than the full vision.

The design principle
Every working session ends with something written. Teachers don't leave empty-handed. The professional learning and the curriculum writing are the same activity.
Teacher Learning — The Problem
Why a template alone isn't enough
A teacher who doesn't understand what a unit is will fill out a unit plan template the way they fill out any form — completing fields without the understanding that makes them meaningful. The professional learning isn't supplemental. It's structural.
What focus groups found
  • Newer teachers often lack a shared mental model of the unit/lesson distinction
  • Instruction tends to be organized at the lesson level — day by day, without a larger arc
  • Teachers who come directly from industry think in tasks and skills, not in units
  • This isn't a knowledge deficit — it's a framing gap that can be addressed
What this means for the work
  • The writing protocol and the professional learning can't be separated
  • The foundational session isn't preparation for the work — it is the beginning of the work
  • Teachers build understanding by doing, not by being trained first and then doing
  • Sustainability requires teachers to own the process, not just follow it
What a unit actually is
  • A coherent chunk of learning organized around a competency cluster or big idea
  • Defined by what students will be able to do independently at the end — not by a chapter or a week
  • Has a beginning, middle, and end — moves students from introduction to independent performance
The CTE bridge
  • Most CTE teachers already have strong intuitions about competency clusters
  • What a journeyman electrician can do is essentially a unit anchor — they just haven't called it that
  • The facilitation move: help teachers see that what they know as trade expertise maps directly onto what a unit builds toward
One way to think about it

The goal isn't to teach teachers a curriculum design framework. It's to help them name and make explicit what they already know as practitioners — and then give that knowledge a structure that can live beyond any individual teacher's classroom.

Teacher Learning — The Arc
What the learning sequence looks like
A four-phase arc from foundational understanding through sustainable ownership. The arc is iterative — phases overlap, and teachers return to earlier phases as new programs come online.
Phase 1
Foundational PD Session
Build shared vocabulary. Establish the unit/lesson relationship using CTE-grounded examples. Review a finished unit exemplar. Preview the writing protocol.
~2–3 hours, facilitated
Phase 2
Competency Mapping
Teachers map what students need to understand and be able to do — organized by program area and aligned to credential frameworks. This becomes the raw material for Stage 1 of each unit.
~2–3 hours
Phase 3
Collaborative Unit Design
Teachers draft units using UbD stages with facilitation support — backward from enduring understanding through performance task to learning plan. PBL delivery designed in from the start. Every session ends with something written.
Multiple sessions, ~2–3 hrs each
Phase 4
Ownership + Sustainability
Teacher leaders take ownership — updating units, onboarding new colleagues, designing new projects. The frameworks become part of how the program thinks, not just what was done once.
Ongoing
One way to think about it

The arc is designed so that at every phase, teachers leave with something real — not a plan for future work, but actual curriculum work product. The learning happens in the doing. That's also what makes it sustainable: teachers who built the curriculum understand it well enough to own it.

Teacher Learning — The Pilot
What a first session could produce
A 2.5–3 hour working session with 2–3 teachers from a pilot program. Low-stakes proof of concept — something real to react to before any larger commitment is made.
Session structure
  • Part 1 (~20 min) — Framing: UbD stages, the unit/lesson distinction, exemplar review
  • Part 2 (~50 min) — Competency mapping: teachers identify what students should understand and be able to do for one program area
  • Part 3 (~60 min) — Unit design: group drafts Stages 1 and 2 for one unit — enduring understanding, essential questions, performance task
  • Part 4 (~20 min) — Debrief and next steps
What comes out of it
  • A draft competency map for the pilot program area — organized by unit
  • A completed Stage 1 and Stage 2 for one unit — enduring understanding, essential questions, performance task
  • A clear picture of what Stage 3 design and full unit writing looks like from here
  • A concrete artifact for the director to react to
What CCIU brings
  • Facilitation for the full session
  • UbD unit plan template and competency mapping framework
  • Documentation of outputs
  • Proposed agenda for follow-on unit design sessions
What to confirm before scheduling
  • Which program area and which campus
  • Which 2–3 teachers — and their availability for a half-day
  • Any relevant credential frameworks or program standards to have on hand
  • Whether PBL delivery is part of the scope for this pilot or a later phase
One way to think about it

The pilot is designed to be low-stakes on purpose. It produces something real without requiring a large commitment upfront. The director gets an artifact to react to; the teachers get a first experience of the process; CCIU gets feedback to refine the approach before scaling.
