The Situation
SREB Audit Findings
What the audit found across all three campuses
  • Curriculum not documented in a consistent format — task sheets functioning as de facto curriculum
  • Same programs taught differently across campuses with no shared scope or sequence
  • No visible alignment to industry credentials or state CTE standards
  • When a teacher leaves, curriculum leaves with them
Focus Group Finding
What teachers told us
  • Newer teachers lack a shared mental model of what a unit is versus a lesson
  • Instruction organized at the lesson level — no larger arc organizing the work
  • A template alone won't fix this — teachers need the conceptual foundation first
→
The Response
Pillar 1
Curriculum Infrastructure
A structured, consistent home for curriculum — templated, mapped to competencies, visible to leadership across all three campuses, designed as a living document.
Pillar 2
Writing Protocol + Teacher Learning
A shared process for how curriculum gets built — with the professional learning designed in from the start, not added on top.
Non-negotiable across both pillars
  • Template structure and competency alignment logic stay consistent regardless of platform chosen
  • The writing protocol and teacher learning are inseparable — one without the other doesn't hold

Option A — Low-Tech
Google Drive / Microsoft 365
A structured folder architecture with standardized naming conventions, shared template files, and a master alignment tracker in a spreadsheet. Everything lives in tools teachers already use.
What it includes
  • Structured folder hierarchy by campus, program, and unit
  • Standardized naming conventions enforced across programs
  • Shared unit plan templates (Google Docs / Word)
  • Master alignment tracker mapping units to competencies (spreadsheet)
  • Shared drive with defined permission levels
Strengths
  • Zero additional cost
  • No learning curve — teachers already use these tools
  • Immediately deployable
  • Easy to onboard new staff
Limitations
  • Structurally fragile — degrades over time without active maintenance discipline
  • No built-in alignment reporting or cross-program visibility
  • Hard to search across programs or campuses at scale
  • Version control is manual and error-prone
Best fit when
  • Budget is unavailable for new tools
  • Goal is to prove the curriculum model before platform investment
  • Speed of deployment is the priority
The honest trade-off

Option A works — but it requires ongoing human discipline to maintain its structure. The value comes from what you put into it, not from the platform itself. If internal capacity to maintain the folder architecture is limited, structure will drift over time. Use Option A as a fast start with a documented intention to migrate once the writing process is established.

Option B — Mid-Tech · Recommended
Notion / Airtable or similar
A relational workspace where units, competencies, and assessments are linked rather than just filed. Dashboards can show alignment at a glance, filter by program or campus, and give leadership a navigable view of curriculum without a spreadsheet.
What it includes
  • Relational database linking units, duty areas, and competencies
  • Views filterable by campus, program, credential, or teacher
  • Leadership dashboard showing curriculum coverage at a glance
  • Embedded unit plan template fields
  • Free tier available; paid tiers modest
Strengths
  • Low cost — free tiers cover many use cases
  • Significantly more powerful than folders — relational structure enables cross-referencing
  • Flexible enough to adapt as the model evolves
  • Visually navigable for both teachers and leadership
Limitations
  • Requires someone to design and maintain the workspace architecture
  • Not purpose-built for curriculum — some structural workarounds needed
  • Adoption requires onboarding even if tools feel intuitive
Best fit when
  • Some internal tech-forward capacity to build and maintain the workspace
  • Want structural power without a full SaaS platform commitment
  • Looking to pilot before committing to a dedicated platform
Why we recommend this as the starting point

Option B gives you most of the structural benefit of a purpose-built platform at a fraction of the cost, with the flexibility to evolve as the curriculum writing process matures. The risk of starting with Option C before the writing process is solid is an expensive empty shell — Option B lets you build the process and demonstrate the value before making a platform investment.

Option C — Purpose-Built Platform
Atlas, Chalk, Toddle, or Curriculum Trak
Dedicated curriculum mapping software designed for exactly this use case — built-in alignment to standards and competency frameworks, cross-campus visibility, teacher-facing curriculum authoring, and leadership reporting.
Platform notes
  • Atlas (Rubicon) — strong UbD alignment, widely used in K–12, good cross-campus reporting
  • Chalk — clean teacher-facing UI, strong CTE competency mapping
  • Toddle — collaborative unit planning, growing US presence
  • Curriculum Trak — purpose-built for CTE, direct competency alignment features
Strengths
  • Highest long-term ceiling
  • Most sustainable — less internal maintenance burden
  • Reporting and alignment visibility built in
  • Purpose-designed for curriculum authoring and management
Limitations
  • Real cost — typically per-seat or institutional licensing
  • Requires onboarding and adoption effort
  • Can feel like overkill if the writing process isn't solid first
  • Vendor dependency
Best fit when
  • Ready to make a platform investment
  • Long-term sustainability and scalability are priorities
  • Leadership wants reporting capability without building it manually
  • Writing process is already established and proven
The honest trade-off

Option C is the right long-term destination — but not necessarily the right starting point. A platform purchased before the curriculum writing process is established becomes an expensive empty shell. The recommendation is to start with Option A or B, build and prove the process, then migrate to a purpose-built platform once the model is demonstrated and the investment is justified.

PL Arc
Foundational PD → Task Analysis → Collaborative Writing → Housing Output. The arc is the same across all three options — what changes is the balance between DACUM task logic and UbD design thinking.
Option A — DACUM-Primary
Task analysis forward
The process starts and largely stays with occupational task analysis. Tasks cluster into units, units map to credentials. UbD concepts — enduring understandings and essential questions — appear as light reflection prompts rather than structural requirements.
How it works
  • Teachers and industry partners surface and validate what a competent worker does
  • Tasks organized by duty area — each duty area becomes a unit anchor
  • Units defined by their task cluster; assessments are performance-based
  • Enduring understandings introduced lightly as "so what" reflection — not a required stage
  • Credential alignment is explicit and primary
Strengths
  • Meets CTE teachers exactly where they already think — in tasks and competencies
  • Familiar for vocational and trades programs
  • Directly tied to credential alignment from the start
  • Fastest path to producing finished unit plans
  • Lower conceptual lift for teachers new to formal curriculum writing
Limitations
  • Can produce units that are thorough but thin on transfer — students learn to do things without understanding why
  • Less satisfying to SREB audit expectations around instructional quality
  • Less defensible as a pedagogical framework in accreditation contexts
Best fit when
  • Teachers are skeptical of education frameworks and need a practical entry point
  • Credential alignment is the primary driver
  • Program is highly skills-intensive — trades, welding, auto tech
  • Speed is the priority and deeper PL investment is not yet feasible
The honest trade-off

Option A is the fastest path and the most comfortable starting point for many CTE teachers. The risk is curriculum that is technically compliant but pedagogically thin — teachers who build units from task lists alone may not develop the conceptual framework needed to update and improve curriculum over time. It works best as a bridge, not a destination.

Option B — DACUM / UbD Hybrid · Recommended
DACUM gives the what. UbD gives the how and why.
Task analysis provides the skeleton — what competent workers do, validated by the field. UbD provides the architecture on top. Each cluster of tasks becomes a unit with an enduring understanding, essential questions, and a defined performance assessment before instruction is planned.
Unit development sequence
  • Task analysis → verified task list by duty area (DACUM)
  • Cluster tasks into units → identify enduring understanding (UbD Stage 1)
  • Define performance assessment and credential alignment (UbD Stage 2)
  • Build instructional sequence backward from assessment (UbD Stage 3)
  • Place finished unit into infrastructure
Strengths
  • Meets teachers where they are (task-based) while building toward something coherent
  • Satisfies both CTE credentialing requirements and instructional quality standards
  • Enduring understandings answer what CTE students most need: "why does this matter in the field?"
  • Defensible in SREB, state, and accreditation contexts
Limitations
  • More facilitation skill required to run well
  • Requires the foundational PL session to work — teachers need the conceptual framework first
  • Takes longer than Option A to produce finished units
Best fit when
  • Goal is curriculum that is both vocationally credible and instructionally coherent
  • SREB audit findings are taken seriously as a driver of change
  • Willing to invest in the PL arc alongside the writing process
  • Recommended for most CTC programs
Why we recommend this approach

The hybrid is the honest best fit for a CTC context. It respects where CTE teachers live intellectually, builds on the DACUM tradition occupational education has trusted for decades, and adds the pedagogical architecture that SREB audits and instructional quality conversations require. It is also the most defensible choice if curriculum quality ever comes under external review — and it produces curriculum teachers actually understand well enough to update and improve over time.

Option C — UbD-Primary
Full backward design with CTE adaptations
Full backward design from the start. Enduring understandings drawn from industry standards and credential frameworks. Essential questions written in industry-relevant language. The process is more formally pedagogical but grounded in CTE context throughout.
How it works
  • Begin with enduring understandings derived from industry standards — not task analysis
  • Essential questions written in program-area language teachers and students recognize
  • Performance assessments defined before any instruction is planned
  • DACUM task logic used as a validity check rather than the starting point
  • Deepest professional learning investment of the three options
Strengths
  • Produces the most pedagogically coherent curriculum
  • Strong alignment with instructional quality frameworks
  • Teachers who engage fully develop the deepest curriculum thinking
  • Most rigorous response to SREB instructional quality findings
Limitations
  • Can feel abstract to trades-oriented teachers without very careful facilitation
  • Highest conceptual lift — requires the most robust foundational PL
  • Risk of alienating teachers skeptical of "education jargon"
  • Slowest path to producing finished unit plans
Best fit when
  • Teachers have prior UbD exposure or academic background
  • Programs are academically adjacent — Health Sciences, IT, Business, Early Childhood
  • Facilitator has strong UbD fluency and CTE contextual knowledge
The honest trade-off

Option C produces the most rigorous curriculum — but carries the highest risk of teacher resistance and the slowest path to finished units. In a CTC context with teachers coming directly from industry, starting here without significant trust-building and facilitation skill can backfire. It is the right destination for programs that have already built some UbD familiarity; it is a difficult starting point from scratch.
