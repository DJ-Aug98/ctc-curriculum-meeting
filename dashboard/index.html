Section 02 · 10 min
What We Know
Evidence-based framing — not a diagnosis request.
Curriculum Documentation
Curriculum is not formally documented in a consistent format
Task sheets and textbooks function as de facto curriculum. No shared understanding of what a "unit" means exists across faculty.
Cross-Campus Consistency
Same programs taught differently across campuses — no shared scope or sequence
No mechanism for teachers across campuses to share, align, or update curriculum. When a teacher leaves, curriculum often leaves with them.
Credential Alignment
Inconsistent or informal alignment to industry credentials
No visible mapping of instruction to state CTE standards, NOCTI, or program competencies. Alignment is implicit rather than documented.
Discovery question

"Of what we've found — what resonates most with what you're seeing from your seat?"

Focus Group Finding

"One thing that came through clearly in focus groups is that newer teachers don't have a shared mental model of what a unit is versus a lesson — they're often working at the lesson level without a larger arc organizing their instruction."

Why this matters

A teacher who doesn't understand what a unit is will fill out a unit plan template like any form — completing fields without the understanding that makes them meaningful. This is why the professional learning component is structural, not optional.

Section 04a · 8 min
Pillar 1 — Curriculum Infrastructure
Where curriculum lives. The structure is non-negotiable. The platform is a decision.
Non-negotiable

Regardless of which option is chosen: the template structure, competency alignment logic, and naming conventions stay consistent. These travel across any platform.

Start with Option A or B — whichever matches your current platform appetite — with a clear intention to migrate to Option C once the writing process is established and the model is proven. Starting with a platform before the process is solid risks an expensive empty shell.

Section 04b · 12 min
Pillar 2 — Writing Protocol + Teacher Learning
Even with a home for curriculum, teachers need a shared process — and the PL to use it.

Option B — the DACUM/UbD hybrid — is our recommendation for a CTC context. It meets CTE teachers where they already live intellectually, builds on the DACUM tradition occupational education has trusted for decades, and adds the pedagogical architecture that SREB and accreditation contexts require.

Option A
DACUM-Primary
Task analysis forward; UbD concepts introduced lightly
  • Starts with occupational task analysis
  • Tasks cluster into units, map to credentials
  • Performance-based assessments
  • Lower conceptual lift
Details ↓
Pros
  • Meets teachers where they think
  • Familiar for vocational programs
  • Fastest to produce units
Cons
  • Can be thin on transfer
  • Less satisfying to SREB expectations
  • Less defensible pedagogically
Best fit: Teachers skeptical of education frameworks; speed is priority; highly skills-intensive programs.
Option BRecommended
DACUM / UbD Hybrid
DACUM gives the what. UbD gives the how and why.
  • Task analysis provides the skeleton
  • UbD architecture on top — enduring understandings, performance assessment
  • Industry-grounded, pedagogically coherent
  • Defensible in SREB + accreditation contexts
Details ↓
Pros
  • Meets teachers where they are
  • Satisfies CTE + instructional quality standards
  • Answers "why does this matter in the field?"
  • Defensible externally
Cons
  • More facilitation skill required
  • Requires foundational PL session first
  • Takes longer than Option A
Best fit: Goal is curriculum that is both vocationally credible and instructionally coherent. Recommended for most CTC programs.
Option C
UbD-Primary
Full backward design with CTE adaptations
  • Enduring understandings from industry standards
  • Essential questions in industry-relevant language
  • Most pedagogically coherent output
  • Highest conceptual lift
Details ↓
Pros
  • Most pedagogically coherent
  • Strong instructional quality alignment
  • Deepest curriculum thinking
Cons
  • Can feel abstract to trades teachers
  • Highest conceptual lift
  • Risk of alienating skeptical teachers
Best fit: Teachers have prior UbD exposure; academically-adjacent programs (Health Sciences, IT, Business).
Design principle

Teachers build understanding by doing the work. The professional learning and the curriculum writing are the same activity — not training followed by writing.

Phase 1
Foundational PD Session
Build shared vocabulary. Establish the unit/lesson relationship. Connect DACUM task analysis to unit architecture. Review the exemplar.
~2–3 hours, facilitated
→
Phase 2
Task Analysis
DACUM-informed session with teachers + industry partners. Generate a verified task inventory organized by duty area. Map to credential frameworks.
~3 hours
→
Phase 3
Collaborative Unit Writing
Teachers draft units using the chosen protocol with facilitation support. Every session ends with something written — no one leaves empty-handed.
Multiple sessions, ~2–3 hrs each
→
Phase 4
Housing + Sustainability
Finished units placed into infrastructure. Teacher leaders take ownership — updating units, onboarding new colleagues, running future task analyses.

Section 05 · 10 min
Reaction + Discussion
"What's landing?
— What questions does this raise?"
Anticipated objections + responses
"Teachers are already stretched."
The PL arc is designed to happen in the context of doing real work — not in addition to it. Teachers leave with actual curriculum, not just training.
"What does this cost?"
Have rough platform cost ranges ready. Process cost is CCIU team time. Offer to follow up with a detailed breakdown.
"How long does this take?"
A pilot through one program can produce finished unit plans within a semester if structured well.
"We already have curriculum somewhere."
Acknowledge it — and reference the audit artifacts as evidence of what "somewhere" looks like in practice.
"Will teachers actually use this?"
The PL arc is designed so teachers build the curriculum themselves — it's theirs, not something handed down.
